I'm John Dougherty, founder and CEO of Island Mountain LLC. We build on-premises AI inference servers specifically for healthcare organizations, and I'd like to discuss a compliance problem that healthcare IT leaders face: HIPAA audit readiness for cloud-based AI.

The HIPAA Security Rule (45 CFR 164.312) specifies technical safeguards that covered entities and business associates must implement. These include access controls, audit controls, integrity controls, and transmission security. When healthcare organizations use cloud AI services, they create structural gaps in these safeguards:

- **Access controls:** PHI is outside the organization's direct control; reliant on cloud provider's access logs
- **Audit logging:** Limited visibility into who accessed PHI at the cloud provider
- **Transmission security:** Data moves over networks the organization doesn't control
- **Integrity:** No direct assurance that PHI wasn't modified

Compliance officers and security teams understand these gaps intuitively. But healthcare organizations haven't had an economically rational alternative—until on-premises AI hardware became viable.

The conversation I'd like to have:
1. A walkthrough of 45 CFR 164.312 technical safeguards and how cloud AI complicates each one

The context is OCAP—Ownership, Control, Access, and Possession. OCAP is the ethical framework that indigenous communities use to assert authority over their own data. It's embedded in tribal data governance policies, tribal research agreements, and assertions of indigenous data sovereignty.

But OCAP has a problem when tribal nations use cloud AI services. The CLOUD Act (Stored Communications Act) gives U.S. law enforcement and foreign governments the power to compel cloud providers to produce data—regardless of where that data is stored. For a tribal nation committed to the Possession principle of OCAP, storing data with a cloud AI provider means that data is subject to seizure by external authorities without tribal consent or tribal involvement.

This isn't hypothetical. I've worked with tribal emergency management departments, tribal health services, and tribal research organizations who understand this issue clearly and are actively seeking alternatives.

The conversation I'd like to have on your show:

No contract, no Service Level Agreement, and no Data Processing Agreement can eliminate this compliance risk. The structural problem remains: external data, external control.

For defense contractors and government agencies, on-premises AI hardware isn't a nice-to-have. It's a compliance requirement.
